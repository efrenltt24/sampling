## Part B - Survey Evaluation:

Identify and describe survey features:

```
1.Sample type: 
    a.Sample survey with a cross-sectional design
    b.The sample is based on a stratified design employing probability sampling. The stratification is done at the province/census metropolitan area (CMA) level.
    c.Information is collected from one randomly selected household member aged 15 or older, and proxy responses are not permitted.

2.Sample size: 
    a. Field sample of approximatively 50,000 units. 
    b.40,000 invitation letters to selected households across Canada. 
    c.A completion of 24,000 questionnaires was expected.

3.Target population: 
    a.All persons 15 years of age and older living in the ten provinces of Canada. 
    b.It excludes full-time (residing for more than six months) residents of institutions.

4.Sampling frame: 
    a.This survey uses a frame that combines landline and cellular telephone numbers from the Census and various administrative sources with Statistics Canada's dwelling frame
    b.Households without telephones, was excluded from the surveyed population

5.Survey mode(s):
    a.Electronic questionnaire or through CATI (computer assisted telephone interviewing).
    b.No proxy reporting was allowed.
    c.The respondents have the choice between French and English.

6.Timeline
    a.Data collection for this reference period: 2018-09-04 to 2018-12-28
    b.Reference period: Past 12 months preceding interview date
    c.Collection period: Every 5 years, from September to December

7.Response rate
    a.The overall response rate is 41.9%. This is the percentage of individuals from a selected sample who actually respond to a survey or data.

8.Weights
    a.WGHT_PER: basic weighting factor for analysis (at the person level). This is a number used to represent a larger group of people in a survey. It helps estimate how many people (aged 15 or older, not living in institutions) have certain characteristics.

    b.Bootstrap weights have been created for the purpose of design-based variance estimation. This helps measure how much the estimates might change due to variations in the data.

9.Data processing
    a.Error detection: Edits were performed automatically and manually at various stages of processing at macro and micro levels. They included family, consistency and flow edits. Family relationships were checked to ensure the integrity of matrix data. Flow edits were used to ensure respondents followed the correct path and fix off-path situations. Error detection was done through edits programmed into the CATI system.

    b.Estimation: The principle behind estimation is that each person selected in the sample represents (in addition to himself/herself) several other persons not in the sample. The number of persons represented by a given respondent is usually known as the weight or weighting factor. the weights were adjusted so that the weighted income distribution of GVP matched the 2017 CIS distribution by province

    c.Disclosure: confidentiality analysis is done using the Statistics Canada Generalized Disclosure Control System (G-Confid).

10.Cleaning, imputation, etc: To address missing or inconsistent responses, the following methods were applied:
    a.Income Data: Income details were collected by linking tax records for respondents who did not object to the linkage. Personal income data from the 2017 T1FF was available for 81.9% of respondents, while missing data for others was imputed. In the 2018 GSS, family income was used instead of household income. Overall, family income data was available for 81.7% of households, and missing values were imputed.

    b.Donor imputation: imputations were made using donor records selected through a score function. Certain characteristics on each record with item or partial nonresponse were compared with the characteristics on all donor records.

    c.Where donor imputation could not be used, mean imputation among a pool of donors was used.

11.	Sources of error
    a.sampling error: Estimates vary between samples and from a full census. More precise estimates of sampling variability were calculated using the bootstrap method. This method was used to measure sampling variability for all estimates based on the 2018 GSS data. Estimates with high variability are marked in this publication, and all highlighted differences between population subgroups are statistically significant at the 95% confidence level.

    b.Non sampling error:
        1.Coverage errors: Coverage errors occur when the surveyed population does not fully match the target population. Households without telephones were not included in the survey. To ensure the results represented everyone in the target population, including those not surveyed, the estimates were adjusted (weighted).

        2.Non-response bias: Information was collected in two stages: at the household level and the individual level. Some households did not respond, and some individuals within responding households also did not participate. Survey estimates will be adjusted (weighted) to compensate for these non-responses. To reduce nonresponse bias, survey weights were adjusted to account for missing responses as much as possible. For the 2018 GSS, another step was added: basic details about non-responding households, like income and family size, were taken from administrative records and used to adjust for nonresponse.
        3.Other types of non-sampling errors can include response errors and processing errors.

12.Limitations, known biases, etc
    a.Lower response rate (41.9%) could introduce nonresponse bias despite imputation.
    b.Surveys conducted by telephone may not fully represent people who do not have phone access.
    c.Survey did not include people in the northern territories.


13.	Link to documentation and any additional sources used
    https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/acs/methodology/sample-size-and-data-quality/response-rates-definitions.html
    https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/acs/methodology/sample-size-and-data-quality/response-rates-definitions.html

```
